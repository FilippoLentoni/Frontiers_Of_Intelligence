\chapter{Building an AI-First Organization}

In modern enterprise product development, the operating model can be represented as one continuous loop:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabularx}{0.92\textwidth}{>{\centering\arraybackslash}X}
\textbf{Business problem identification}
$\rightarrow$ \textbf{BRD}
$\rightarrow$ \textbf{HLD}
$\rightarrow$ \textbf{LLD} \\
$\downarrow$ \\
\textbf{Asana tasks}
$\rightarrow$ \textbf{implementation}
$\rightarrow$ \textbf{execution}
$\rightarrow$ \textbf{feedback} \\
$\downarrow$ \\
\textbf{new tasks or updated business problem/design}
\end{tabularx}
\end{center}

In an AI-first company, automation follows an \emph{AI gradient}. The center of the loop is the most automatable because it is more technical, structured, and repeatable. The edges of the loop remain the most human-intensive because they require judgment, ambiguity resolution, and accountability.

At the beginning of the loop, humans identify the business problem, set the priority, and define the expected outcome. This is where human expertise is hardest to replace: identifying the right problem before it is fully specified, inferring what is unspoken, anticipating risks before they appear, and recognizing opportunities that are not yet obvious from the data.

As work moves toward the center of the loop, AI can take on more of the translation and implementation. It can help convert a BRD into an HLD, an HLD into an LLD, and an LLD into Asana tasks. The coding agent then implements the required software changes through the software factory. In this context, the software factory is the set of skills, tools, tests, and CI/CD mechanisms that allow coding agents to generate software compliant with company requirements.

The system leverages feedback signals such as SME audits through sampling, customer feedback, and actuals data. These signals are converted into metrics that compare current performance against the ideal state. Based on the gap, the AI agent decides the next action. If the gap is small or localized, it creates a new Asana task. If the gap suggests a deeper issue in the business logic or system design, it brings the problem back to the SME and proposes updates to the business problem, BRD, HLD, or LLD.

\section{Minimum Architecture}

The minimum architecture requires three components:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=0pt,style=nextline]
\item[Software factory.]
The software factory is the engine that converts structured tasks into production-grade software. It uses coding agents, tests, CI/CD, and deployment gates to generate changes that are compliant with company requirements.

\item[MCP interfaces.]
The organization needs a small set of interfaces that allow humans and AI to communicate through the systems where work already happens. In a first version, these interfaces can connect to Asana, Outlook, and Slack. Asana acts as the execution surface, while Outlook and Slack act as communication surfaces.

\item[Knowledge base.]
The knowledge base stores the key documents and context required by the AI system. This includes BRDs, HLDs, LLDs, system context, ownership information, and relevant operating assumptions.
\end{description}

As AI technology becomes more commoditized, the main competitive advantage will come from three sources: proprietary data, investment capacity, and grounding in the physical world. Proprietary data gives the AI system context that competitors cannot easily copy. Investment capacity allows the company to build the software factory, evaluation loops, and infrastructure required to scale. Grounding in the physical world creates a path to monetization.
